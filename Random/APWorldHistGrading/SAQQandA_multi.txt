CATEGORY: SAQ

Question1
(a)
Q: Briefly describe ONE cause of the Columbian Exchange.
A: The Columbian Exchange was caused primarily by Christopher Columbus's 1492 voyage to the Americas, which established sustained contact between the Eastern and Western Hemispheres for the first time in recorded history. European imperial expansion, driven by the desire for new trade routes and resources, created the transatlantic networks through which plants, animals, diseases, and people flowed.

(b)
Q: Briefly explain ONE effect of the Columbian Exchange on the Americas.
A: One significant effect of the Columbian Exchange on the Americas was the catastrophic demographic collapse of indigenous populations. European diseases such as smallpox, measles, and influenza, to which Native Americans had no prior immunity, wiped out an estimated 50–90% of indigenous peoples within a century of contact. This demographic disaster disrupted existing political structures, economies, and cultures, enabling European colonization.

(c)
Q: Briefly explain ONE effect of the Columbian Exchange on Europe or Africa.
A: One major effect of the Columbian Exchange on Europe was the introduction of new crops such as potatoes, maize, and tomatoes from the Americas. These crops, particularly the potato in Ireland and Northern Europe, dramatically increased caloric availability, contributing to population growth in the 17th and 18th centuries and helping fuel the labor supply needed for eventual industrialization.

Question2
(a)
Q: Describe one change that resulted from Zheng He's voyages in the Indian Ocean in the period 1400–1450.
A: One change resulting from Zheng He's voyages was the expansion of Chinese diplomatic influence across Southeast Asia, South Asia, and East Africa. By presenting lavish gifts and demanding tribute from rulers along the route, the Ming Dynasty used these voyages to project imperial power and bring dozens of states into the Chinese tributary system, reshaping the political landscape of the Indian Ocean world.

(b)
Q: Describe one continuity in Indian Ocean trade in the period 1200–1450.
A: One continuity in Indian Ocean trade from 1200 to 1450 was the reliance on monsoon winds to drive maritime commerce. Merchants from Arabia, India, East Africa, and Southeast Asia had long coordinated their voyages with the seasonal reversal of the monsoon, sailing northeast in winter and southwest in summer. This pattern persisted throughout the period, structuring the rhythm of trade regardless of which political powers dominated the routes.

(c)
Q: Explain one way the decision to end Zheng He's voyages impacted China in the period 1450–1750.
A: The end of Zheng He's voyages led to China's gradual withdrawal from active participation in Indian Ocean maritime trade, creating a power vacuum that European powers — particularly Portugal, then the Netherlands — were able to exploit. Without Chinese naval presence, European merchants established fortified trading posts along the same routes Zheng He had sailed, ultimately reshaping Indian Ocean commerce in ways that disadvantaged Chinese merchants and reduced Ming influence in the region.

Question3
(a)
Q: Identify one similarity in the administrative techniques used by two different imperial states in the period 1200–1450.
A: Both the Mongol Empire and the Ottoman Empire relied on a loyal, centrally controlled military corps to maintain imperial authority. The Mongols used personally loyal generals appointed by the khan, while the Ottomans developed the devshirme system, conscripting Christian boys who were converted to Islam and trained as elite soldiers (Janissaries) answerable only to the sultan. In both cases, the empire deliberately bypassed traditional local elites to build a military force whose loyalty was to the central authority rather than to regional nobles or clans.

(b)
Q: Explain one reason why imperial states in the period 1200–1450 used centralized administrative techniques.
A: Imperial states adopted centralized administration because it allowed rulers to project power over vast, diverse territories without relying on potentially disloyal local lords. By appointing officials who owed their position to the emperor or sultan rather than to hereditary status, rulers could collect taxes, enforce laws, and mobilize armies across wide geographic areas. The Song Dynasty's civil service examination system, for example, created a bureaucratic class of merit-selected officials loyal to the emperor, enabling effective governance over a large and complex population.

(c)
Q: Identify one difference between the political systems of imperial states and those of decentralized regions in the period 1200–1450.
A: A key difference was the concentration of authority. Imperial states such as the Mali Empire and the Byzantine Empire maintained centralized hierarchies with a single paramount ruler who controlled taxation, military mobilization, and law across broad territories. In contrast, decentralized regions such as the feudal kingdoms of Western Europe distributed power among competing lords, each controlling their own lands and armies, with the monarch holding only nominal authority. This fragmentation made coordinated large-scale action — such as raising a national army or building major infrastructure — far more difficult in decentralized systems.
